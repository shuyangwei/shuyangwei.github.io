excerpt: 'This paper compares the basic passive expressions between Malay and Chinese from a synchronic perspective, finding that the passive meanings are both expressed by topic structures and passive structures in these two languages. Topic structures and passive structures have their own pragmatic functions in expressing passive meanings. The underlying logic is that semantic functions and pragmatic functions influence syntactic structures. In contrast to Chinese, passive expressions in Malay also differ in forms due to the differences in personal pronouns. When the agent is a first personal pronoun or a second personal pronoun, the patient in Malay is preposed without any passive markers. This is similar to the topic clauses in Chinese. When the agent is a third personal pronoun or a noun, dominant passive markers are used in Malay, such as inflectional verbs. We also sort out passive expressions in Sino-Tibetan languages from a linguistic typological perspective, concluding that topic structures and passive structures coexist in passive expressions is a common phenomenon.'
