excerpt: 'This paper compares the basic passives between Malay and Mandarin Chinese, finding that the passives are both expressed by topic structures and passive structures in the two languages. Topic structures and passive structures have their own pragmatic functions in expressing passive meanings. The underlying logic is that semantic functions and pragmatic functions appeal to syntactic structures. In contrast to Mandarin Chinese, passives in Malay differ in forms due to different personal pronouns. When the agent is a first or a second personal pronoun, the patient in Malay is preposed without any passive markers. This is similar to the topic structures in Mandarin Chinese. When the agent is a third personal pronoun or a noun, dominant passive markers are used in Malay, such as inflectional verbs. We also sort out passives in Sino-Tibetan languages from the perspective of typology, concluding that topic structures and passive structures coexist in passives is universal cross-linguistically.'
